# Progress Report — Faithful Structured Extraction from Oncology Notes with a Local LLM

## Abstract

We are building a system that turns free-text oncology clinic notes into faithful, structured clinical fields (current anticancer medications, stage, metastasis, treatment response, receptor/molecular status, and care plans) so the information can be summarized for patients without hallucination or privacy leakage. Rather than fine-tuning, our approach is an **inference harness around a frozen, locally served open-weight model** (Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct-AWQ via vLLM): multi-stage extraction, a separate five-gate verification cascade, oncology drug/term dictionaries, and ~135 deterministic clinical post-processing rules — all on-premises and HIPAA-relevant. On 40 expert-annotated held-out notes (20 breast, 20 pancreatic), we ran a controlled ablation holding the base model and field schema fixed so the harness is the only variable. Under a **defined LLM-assisted, field-level review protocol** (these are automated, pre-physician estimates), the harness (PL) beats the same model run from a single prompt (BL) on **131 of 618** field comparisons with **12** losses and the rest ties; among the fields where the two systems disagree, **PL wins 90%** and never loses a core diagnostic field. A **blinded physician scoring study is the confirmatory next step and has already been sent out**. Earlier physician review of a patient-letter version flagged over-simplification, which led us to refocus the period on the objectively-measurable extraction task.

## Accomplishments (this reporting period)

**Methods built.** A complete, reproducible extraction pipeline ("PL") over a frozen open model, with **no weight training by design** (keeps data local, needs no labeled corpus, stays reproducible). The system is an *inference harness* — a fixed base model plus deterministic routing and rules. Adaptation is entirely inference-time, in four layers:

- **(i) Multi-stage extraction** — the note is read in stages, not one prompt. *Stage 1*: six independent prompts (reason for visit; diagnosis with type/stage/metastasis; labs; exam & imaging findings; current medications; treatment changes). *Stage 2*: two prompts that depend on Stage 1 and receive its output as context (treatment goal; treatment response). *Stage 3*: plan extraction (medication / procedure / imaging / lab / genetic-test / referral / follow-up).
- **(ii) Five-gate verification cascade** — a *separate* layer applied to each extracted field **after** extraction (it is not one of the extraction stages): format-repair → schema/key-check → specificity & semantic-alignment → faithfulness-trim → temporal-filter.
- **(iii) Two deterministic dictionaries** — ~148 oncology drugs; 18,739 medical terms at an 8th-grade reading level.
- **(iv) ~135 deterministic post-processing rules**, each firing only when its clinical trigger condition matches (e.g., regional-vs-distant nodes, chemo-hold counts as a plan change, receptor consistency).

**Development & physician-preference loop.** Development used an evaluation-driven loop on 200 unannotated notes (~15 breast / ~18 pancreatic rounds), preferring generalizable clinical rules over test-set hard-coding. The loop differed by cancer: for **breast**, an oncologist reviewed outputs and that feedback was relayed into the same working session and supplied to the LLM judge as context, so the judge captured the physician's preferences; for **PDAC**, iteration was then driven by that preference-informed LLM judge alone, **with no physician input**, and still yielded PL > BL — evidence the captured preferences carried over.

**Evaluation method (important caveat).** All current verdicts are from an **LLM-as-judge protocol, not human review**: the 40 samples are split across several LLM review agents running in parallel (each agent reviews a subset of the samples). Each agent is briefed with the clinical rubric (the four principles, P0/P1/P2 severity, per-field definitions, "extraction not summary"), reads the *full* source note plus both systems' output, and judges each field in natural language (no scripts/keyword matching); a separate orchestrating model then re-checks every PL loss and contested verdict against the note. This LLM-as-judge design follows established healthcare practice — a scoping review of LLM-as-a-Judge methods and human alignment in healthcare [1] and a clinical-summary study validating LLM judges against a human instrument (PDSQI) with strong inter-rater reliability [2] — both reporting moderate-to-strong LLM–expert agreement. The numbers are therefore **automated, pre-physician estimates**; the blinded physician study (next step, already sent out) is what will confirm them.

**Key outcomes.**
- **Controlled ablation (40 held-out notes), PL vs single-prompt baseline (BL), same base model + schema; LLM-assisted review:** PL better **131** / tie **475** / BL better **12** out of 618 field comparisons; among the fields where the two systems differ, **PL is better in 90%** and **never loses on a core diagnostic field** (Figure 1). We define **core diagnostic fields** as the high-value clinical fields that require oncology knowledge to get right: current anticancer medications, cancer stage, distant metastasis, regional metastasis, treatment response, type/receptor status, and molecular/genetic results. The strongest single separation is current-medication "anticancer vs non-cancer home-med" identification (**33 wins : 0 losses**); stage, distant metastasis, and molecular/genetic results are also PL-dominant.
- **Deployment safety (same base model, naive prompt vs harness, 40 letters):** prompted naively the model was **0/40 sendable** (45% leaked de-identification placeholders, 12.5% hallucination); inside the harness the same model reached **zero hallucination and zero leaked redaction, fully on-prem** — i.e., the harness, not a larger model, is what makes the open model safe to use on real notes.
- **Held-out extraction accuracy (breast):** P0 (hallucination) = 0, P1 = 0; receptor status 93%, treatment-goal direction 100%.

**Impact.** The result shows that a frozen, open, *locally hosted* model — unusable for this task when prompted naively — becomes clinically reliable purely through an inference harness. This is directly relevant to clinical deployment, where data cannot leave the institution and model fine-tuning is often infeasible; the same recipe transfers to other note types without retraining.

**Cancer-relevant impact.** Every high-value field the harness fixes is oncology-specific and decision-relevant: correctly separating active anticancer regimens from home medications, assigning AJCC stage, distinguishing regional nodal involvement from distant metastasis, and surfacing molecular results (e.g., BRCA, MMR/MSI, CA19-9 non-secretor). Accurate, faithful extraction of these is the prerequisite for safe patient communication and for downstream cohort/registry use in breast and pancreatic cancer.

## Goals for the next reporting period

1. **Blinded clinician scoring (in progress, already sent out):** the A/B-blinded scoring instrument (system identity hidden) has been handed to oncologist(s); collect and analyze their independent, quantitative PL-vs-BL win rate and compare it against the LLM-assisted estimates reported here.
2. **Generalization audit:** confirm the ~135 post-processing rules encode general clinical logic (not test-set artifacts) and validate on fresh notes / additional cancer types.
3. **Determinism:** reduce greedy-decode run-to-run variation so final metrics are stable.
4. **Accuracy-first summarization:** reintroduce patient-facing output under an explicit "accuracy over simplification" constraint.
5. **Write-up:** consolidate the ablation + deployment results into a publishable "inference-harness for reliable extraction on a frozen local LLM" report.

## Discussion

Most fields tie because both systems share the same strong base model; the harness's measurable value concentrates precisely in the high-knowledge fields where a naive prompt is systematically wrong — exactly the fields a clinician cares about. The few BL "wins" are minor omissions, not correctness failures, and several were further reduced this period by a prompt change that pushes the model to *extract concrete facts rather than infer tidy summaries*. The chief caveat is that the numbers above are **LLM-as-judge, not physician-confirmed**; we mitigate this with a rubric-constrained protocol and an orchestrator verification pass, but the blinded oncologist study now out for review is what will validate them. A secondary limitation is run-to-run nondeterminism from greedy decoding, which produces long-tail variation we now control with deterministic rules. The pivot from patient letters to extraction was driven by physician feedback and by the need for an objective, controlled metric; with extraction now demonstrably reliable, accuracy-constrained summarization is the natural next step.
